COVID-19 Variants

What is a variant?

In the medical field, a variant is described as a change in the DNA sequence of a cell. Variants are a mutation of a cell's genetic code. Some of the variants may cause a new cell or tissue type. Many variants have been found in people. The changes in their code can increase the virus' lifespan, transmissibility, resistance to medicines, the severity of disease, etc. According to the National Human Genome Research Institute (NHGRI) of the USA, there are more than 10 million variants in our genetic code that can be harmful or harmless.

The majority of harmless mutations may increase one's risk of developing a particular disease, while the effects of harmful mutations are usually worse. For example, a variant can result in changes in proteins that can alter their function and lead to other diseases. Another example is that if the particular gene is related to cancer cells, the variant will increase one's risk for cancer or worsen their cancer prognosis. Studies have revealed that most variants don't cause damage but instead offer new advantages to prevent diseases such as malaria and sickle cell anemia.

Beta

The beta variant, also known as (B.1.351), is a variant of SARS-CoV-2. One of several SARS-CoV-2 variants deemed particularly important was first detected in the Nelson Mandela Bay metropolitan area in the Eastern Cape Province of South Africa in October 2020, which was reported by the Department of the country's health on 18 Dec 2020.

There are three mutations of particular interest in the peak region of the genome of the lineage B.1.351: K417N, E484K, and N501Y. E484K might help the virus evade a person's immune system, and it may affect how vaccines work. Other less common mutations include L18F, D80A, D215G, R246I, A701V.

The World Health Organization has labeled the variant a beta variant, not to replace the scientific name, but as a name that the public can commonly refer to.

Gamma

The Gamma variant (P.1) is one of the variants of SARS-CoV-2. This variant has three common mutations, namely: N501Y, E484K, and K417T. This SARS-CoV-2 variant was first detected by Japan's National Institute of Infectious Diseases (NIID) on January 6, 2021, in four people who arrived in Tokyo after visiting Amazonas, Brazil 4 days earlier. It was later declared in circulation in Brazil.

The World Health Organization labeled the Gamma Variant as P.1 for simplicity and was declared a variant of concern. Gamma caused a widespread infection in early 2021 in the city of Manaus, the capital of the Amazon.

The Gamma variant includes the two distinct subvariants 28-AM-1 and 28-AM-2, which carry the two mutations K417T, E484K, N501Y, and both developed independently of each other in the same Brazilian Amazon region.

The Delta version became named on 31 May 2021 and had unfolded to over 179 international locations through 22 November 2021. The World Health Organization (WHO) indicated in June 2021 that the Delta variant is turning into the dominant pressure globally.

This variant has mutations withinside the gene encoding the SARS-CoV-2 spike protein. Omicron

The Omicron variant (B.1.1.529) is a variant of SARS-CoV-2 and was first reported from South Africa on November 24, 2021. Omicron multiplies 70 times faster than the Delta variant, but evidence suggests it is less severe than previous strains, especially compared to the Delta variant. Overall, the very high prevalence rate, combined with its ability to evade both double vaccination and the body's immune system, indicates that the total number of patients requiring hospital care at any given time is always a source of concern.

Vaccines continue to protect against serious illness and hospitalization, especially after the administration of the third dose of an mRNA vaccine. Double vaccination provides 30-40% protection against infection and about 70% against hospitalization. A recent third dose of the vaccine increases effectiveness against infections to around 75% and 88% for serious illnesses.
